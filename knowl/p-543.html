Case I. When roots (\(k_{1}{,}k_{2}\)) are distinct and not differing by an integer, then the complete solution is
\begin{equation*} y=C_{1}(y)_{k_{1}}+C_{2}(y)_{k_{2}} \end{equation*}
Case II. When roots are equal (\(k_{1}=k_{2}\)), then
\begin{equation*} y=C_{1}(y)_{k_{1}}+C_{2}(\frac{dy}{dk})_{k_{1}}. \end{equation*}
Case III. When roots are distinct and differ by an integer, then there arise two possibilities
  1. If some of the coefficients of y series become infinite at \(k=k_{2} \text{,}\) then replace \(a_{o}\) by \(b_{o}(k-k_{2})\text{.}\) The complete solution is then
    \begin{equation*} y=C_{1}(y)_{k_{1}}+C_{2}(\frac{dy}{dk})_{k_{2}} \end{equation*}
    [on taking \(k=k_{1}\text{,}\) we obtain a solution which is a constant multiple of that obtained for \(k_{1}\text{.}\)]
  2. When the coefficient of y series does not become infinite for any \(k\text{,}\) then the complete solution is
    \begin{equation*} y=C_{1}(y)_{k_{1}}+C_{2}(y)_{k_{2}} \end{equation*}
